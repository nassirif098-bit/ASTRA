Uranus

The Ice Giant - Sideways Planet

About Uranus

What is Uranus?

Uranus is the seventh planet from the Sun and the third-largest in our Solar System. It's classified as an ice giant, with a unique feature - it rotates on its side, with an axial tilt of 98 degrees, making its seasons extreme and unusual.

Composition

Uranus is composed primarily of ices (water, ammonia, methane) and rock, with a hydrogen and helium atmosphere. The planet's blue-green color comes from methane in its atmosphere, which absorbs red light and reflects blue-green light.

Did You Know?

Uranus was the first planet discovered with a telescope by William Herschel in 1781. The planet has 13 known rings and 27 moons, all named after characters from the works of Shakespeare and Alexander Pope.

Structure of Uranus

Uranus has a unique layered structure:

  • Core : Small rocky core about 0.55 Earth masses
  • Mantle : Icy mantle of water, ammonia, and methane ices
  • Atmosphere : Hydrogen, helium, and methane gas envelope
  • Ring System : 13 faint rings composed of dark particles
  • Magnetic Field : Tilted and offset from planet's center

The Sideways Planet

Uranus has the most extreme axial tilt in the Solar System at 98 degrees, meaning it essentially orbits the Sun on its side. This unusual orientation gives Uranus extreme seasons - each pole gets about 42 years of continuous sunlight followed by 42 years of darkness.

Scientists believe this extreme tilt was caused by a collision with an Earth-sized protoplanet early in the Solar System's history.

Unique Features

  • Axial Tilt : 98 degrees - rotates on its side
  • Blue-Green Color : Caused by methane in atmosphere
  • Rings : 13 faint rings discovered in 1977
  • Moons : 27 known moons with Shakespearean names
  • Ice Giant : Classification shared only with Neptune
  • Magnetic Field : Tilted 59 degrees from rotation axis

Uranus Exploration Timeline

1781

William Herschel discovers Uranus, the first planet found with a telescope, originally naming it "Georgian Star" after King George III.

1787

William Herschel discovers Uranus's two largest moons, Titania and Oberon.

1977

Astronomers discover Uranus's ring system during stellar occultation observations from the Kuiper Airborne Observatory.

1986

Voyager 2 becomes the only spacecraft to visit Uranus, flying within 81,500 km and discovering 10 new moons.

1997-2005

Hubble Space Telescope observations reveal seasonal changes and discover two additional outer rings.

2003-2005

Keck Observatory using adaptive optics discovers small inner moons and provides detailed images of ring system.
