Jupiter

The Giant Planet - King of the Solar System

About Jupiter

What is Jupiter?

Jupiter is the fifth planet from the Sun and the largest in our Solar System. It's a gas giant with a mass one-thousandth that of the Sun, but two-and-a-half times that of all the other planets in the Solar System combined.

Composition

Jupiter is primarily composed of hydrogen (90%) and helium (10%), with traces of methane, water vapor, ammonia, and silicon-based compounds. The planet likely has a solid core of heavier elements beneath its dense atmospheric layers.

Did You Know?

Jupiter's Great Red Spot is a gigantic storm that has been raging for at least 400 years. The planet has 95 known moons, including Ganymede - the largest moon in our Solar System, even bigger than Mercury.

Structure of Jupiter

Jupiter has a complex layered structure:

  • Core : Possible rocky core 10-20 times Earth's mass
  • Metallic Hydrogen : Layer where hydrogen acts as electrical conductor
  • Liquid Hydrogen : Thick layer of molecular hydrogen
  • Atmosphere : Cloud bands of ammonia, ammonium hydrosulfide, and water
  • Magnetosphere : Largest and most powerful in Solar System

The Gas Giant

Jupiter is so massive that its barycenter with the Sun lies above the Sun's surface. The planet's rapid rotation causes it to have the shape of an oblate spheroid, with a noticeable bulge around the equator.

Jupiter's strong magnetic field is 14 times stronger than Earth's, creating intense radiation belts that make close exploration extremely challenging for spacecraft.

Unique Features

  • Great Red Spot : Massive anticyclonic storm larger than Earth
  • Cloud Bands : Colorful atmospheric bands created by strong zonal winds
  • Magnetosphere : Extends 7 million kilometers toward the Sun
  • Rings : Faint ring system discovered in 1979
  • Moons : Extensive system including four large Galilean moons
  • Auroras : Permanent auroral displays at both poles

Jupiter Exploration Timeline

1610

Galileo Galilei discovers Jupiter's four largest moons: Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto.

1973

Pioneer 10 becomes first spacecraft to fly by Jupiter, returning close-up images.

1979

Voyager 1 and 2 discover Jupiter's rings, volcanic activity on Io, and detailed structure of Great Red Spot.

1995

Galileo spacecraft arrives at Jupiter, deploying probe into atmosphere and orbiting for 8 years.

2016

Juno spacecraft arrives at Jupiter, beginning detailed study of planet's interior and magnetosphere.

2023

Juno completes primary mission, continues extended operations revealing new insights about Jupiter's storms.
