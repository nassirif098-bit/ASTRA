About Venus

What is Venus?

Venus is Earth's sister planet, similar in size and composition but vastly different in environment. It's shrouded in thick clouds of sulfuric acid and has a runaway greenhouse effect that makes it the hottest planet in our solar system.

Composition

Venus has a rocky composition similar to Earth, with an iron core, rocky mantle, and crust. Its thick atmosphere is primarily carbon dioxide (96.5%) with clouds of sulfuric acid.

Did You Know?

Venus rotates backwards compared to other planets, and a day on Venus (243 Earth days) is longer than its year (225 Earth days). It's the only planet named after a female goddess.

Structure of Venus

Venus shares a similar internal structure with Earth:

  • Core : Iron-nickel core about 3,000 km in diameter
  • Mantle : Rocky mantle of silicate materials
  • Crust : Solid surface with volcanoes, mountains, and vast plains
  • Atmosphere : Dense atmosphere creating extreme greenhouse effect

Extreme Environment

Venus has the hottest surface of any planet at 471°C (880°F), hot enough to melt lead. The atmospheric pressure is 92 times that of Earth, equivalent to being 900 meters underwater.

Its thick clouds reflect about 75% of sunlight, making Venus the brightest natural object in the night sky after the Moon.

Surface Features

  • Volcanoes : Over 1,600 major volcanoes, some possibly active
  • Maxwell Montes : Highest mountains, taller than Mount Everest
  • Impact Craters : Fewer craters due to volcanic resurfacing
  • Lava Plains : Vast plains covered by volcanic lava flows
  • Coronae : Circular crown-like features from mantle plumes

Venus Timeline

4.5 Billion Years Ago

Venus forms from the solar nebula, potentially with oceans like Earth.

1 Billion Years Ago

Runaway greenhouse effect begins, boiling away any oceans and creating current extreme conditions.

1962

Mariner 2 becomes first successful mission to Venus, confirming its extreme surface temperature.

1975-1982

Soviet Venera missions successfully land on Venus and transmit the first images from its surface.

1990-1994

Magellan orbiter maps 98% of Venus's surface using radar to penetrate thick clouds.
