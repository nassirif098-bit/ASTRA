EXOPLANETS

Worlds Beyond Our Solar System

What are Exoplanets?

Exoplanets, or extrasolar planets, are planets that orbit stars other than our Sun. Since the first confirmed detection in 1992, we've discovered over 5,500 of these alien worlds, each with unique characteristics. Some are scorching hot gas giants, while others are frozen ice worlds. A few might even harbor the conditions necessary for life.

Super-Earths

Rocky planets larger than Earth but smaller than Neptune. They could be rocky, icy, or gaseous, and some orbit in the habitable zone.

  • Mass 1.5 to 10 times Earth
  • Could have thick atmospheres
  • Some in habitable zones
  • Example: Kepler-452b

Neptune-like

Ice giants similar to Neptune and Uranus, with hydrogen-helium atmospheres and possibly icy cores beneath thick clouds.

  • Smaller than Jupiter
  • Rich in water, ammonia, methane
  • Most common type discovered
  • Example: HAT-P-11b

Terrestrial

Rocky planets similar to Earth, Mars, and Venus. These are the most exciting for the search for extraterrestrial life.

  • Similar composition to Earth
  • Solid surfaces
  • Prime candidates for life
  • Example: TRAPPIST-1e
üåë

Ocean Worlds

Hypothetical planets completely covered in water, with oceans hundreds of kilometers deep. Life might exist in these alien seas.

  • Entirely covered in water
  • Could have subsurface oceans
  • Potential for aquatic life
  • Example: Kepler-22b (candidate)

Rogue Planets

Planets that don't orbit any star, drifting alone through interstellar space. They were ejected from their original systems.

  • No host star
  • Extremely cold and dark
  • Hard to detect
  • Example: CFBDSIR 2149-0403

How We Detect Exoplanets

01

Transit Method

When a planet passes in front of its star, it causes a tiny dip in the star's brightness. By measuring these dips, we can determine the planet's size and orbital period.

~75% of discoveries
02

Radial Velocity

A planet's gravity causes its star to wobble slightly. This wobble shifts the star's light spectrum, which we can detect. This reveals the planet's mass and orbit.

~20% of discoveries
03

Direct Imaging

Using powerful telescopes to actually photograph exoplanets. This is extremely difficult because stars are much brighter than planets.

~2% of discoveries
04

Gravitational Microlensing

When a planet passes in front of a distant star, its gravity bends the starlight like a lens, creating a characteristic brightness pattern.

~2% of discoveries
